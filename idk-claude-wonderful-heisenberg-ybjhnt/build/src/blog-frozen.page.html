How to keep your pipes from freezing in a Regina winter

By the Wascana Plumbing crew · Updated November 2025 · 9 min read

Regina gets cold in a way that surprises even seasoned prairie folks — a −30 °C snap with a −45 windchill isn't rare, it's January. And every year, those cold snaps split open pipes in homes all over the city. The good news: frozen pipes are one of the most preventable plumbing disasters there is. Here's exactly how we tell our own neighbours to get ready.

Why pipes freeze (and why they burst)

Water expands by about 9% when it freezes. When a slug of ice forms in a pipe, it doesn't usually split the pipe at the ice itself — it traps water between the ice and a closed tap, and the pressure builds until the weakest point lets go. That's why a pipe can "freeze" overnight and only burst the next afternoon when it thaws. By then you might have water pouring through a ceiling.

In Regina, three things make it worse than average: long stretches of extreme cold, a lot of older homes with uninsulated crawlspaces and exterior walls, and the prairie wind, which finds every gap in your foundation and drives cold air straight onto your pipes.

The 30-second version: keep heat moving to where the pipes are, seal the cold air out, and on the worst nights let a tap trickle. If you do nothing else, do those three.

The spots most likely to freeze in a prairie home

Frozen pipes almost always happen in the same handful of places. Walk your house and check each one:

  • Exterior walls under kitchen and bathroom sinks — especially on the north and west sides that take the wind.
  • Outdoor hose bibs (sillcocks) and the pipe feeding them through the rim joist.
  • Crawlspaces, cold cellars and uninsulated basement corners.
  • The rim joist / header where the foundation meets the floor framing — a classic Regina cold spot.
  • Garage walls with plumbing in them, if the garage isn't heated.
  • Pipes running through an attic or a porch that was finished later without proper insulation.

9 things to do before the next cold snap

1. Disconnect and drain your garden hoses

This is the single most common cause of a burst we see in the fall. A hose left attached traps water in the sillcock, it freezes back into the wall, and the pipe splits where you can't see it. Disconnect every hose, drain it, and if you have an interior shut-off for the outdoor tap, close it and open the outside spigot to drain.

2. Seal the air leaks near pipes

Cold air, not cold temperatures alone, is what freezes pipes. Find the drafts around your rim joist, foundation cracks, dryer vents and where cables or pipes enter the house, and seal them with caulk or expanding foam. A $12 can of foam has saved a lot of Regina basements.

3. Insulate the pipes you can reach

Foam pipe sleeves from any hardware store slip right over exposed pipes in the basement, crawlspace and garage. They're cheap, they take an afternoon, and they make a real difference on the lines that run along cold exterior walls.

4. Know where your main shut-off is — and that it works

If a pipe does let go, the difference between a mop-up and a flood is how fast you can shut the water off. Your main valve is usually in the basement near where the water line enters, often by the meter. Find it now, make sure everyone in the house knows it, and turn it off and on once to confirm it isn't seized.

5. Open cabinet doors on the cold nights

Sinks on exterior walls get their warmth from your room air. On a deep-freeze night, open the cabinet doors under those sinks so heat can reach the pipes. (If you've got kids or pets, move any cleaners out first.)

6. Let a tap trickle when it's brutal

On nights below about −30 °C, let the faucet farthest from your main run a pencil-thin trickle of cold water. Moving water is far harder to freeze, and the open tap relieves the pressure that actually causes bursts. A few cents of water beats a few thousand in damage.

7. Keep the heat on — even when you're away

Heading to somewhere warm for February? Never set the furnace below 13 °C, and ideally leave it at 16–18 °C. Have someone check the house every couple of days. A furnace that quits while you're gone is how a single cold weekend wrecks a whole basement.

8. Service the furnace before winter

Your heating system is your pipes' best friend. A furnace that fails at −35 °C turns into a frozen-pipe emergency within hours. Get it inspected in the fall, change the filter, and keep the area around vents clear so warm air actually reaches the basement.

9. Add heat to the trouble spots

For a crawlspace or a cold corner that freezes every year, a thermostatically-controlled heat cable wrapped on the pipe, or a small amount of safe supplemental heat in the space, fixes it permanently. We can spec the right setup if you're not sure.

What to do if a pipe freezes anyway

Caught it before it burst? Move fast but stay calm:

  1. Open the tap the frozen pipe feeds, so water can escape as the ice melts and pressure can release.
  2. Find the frozen section — it'll often be frosty or bulging, usually against an exterior wall.
  3. Warm it gently with a hair dryer, a space heater kept a safe distance away, or towels soaked in hot water. Start nearest the tap and work back toward the cold spot.
  4. Never use an open flame. A blowtorch on a pipe is how house fires start, and it can crack the pipe. Don't.
  5. Watch for the split. If water comes pouring out as it thaws, the pipe already cracked — shut off the main and call us.
If you can't reach the frozen section, the pipe has already burst, or you're not confident — stop and call. A frozen pipe is a lot cheaper to deal with than a flooded basement.

When to call a plumber

Call right away if you have no water at all in deep cold (likely a frozen main or meter), if you can see or hear water escaping, if a pipe is bulging or split, or if the frozen section is somewhere you can't safely reach. A quick word on the spring melt

Once you've survived the freeze, the next prairie threat is the thaw. Regina's flat clay doesn't drain quickly, and a fast March melt can push water toward your foundation and up through basement drains. Before spring, test your sump pump by pouring a bucket of water into the pit to make sure it kicks on, and consider a backwater valve if your basement has ever backed up.
